The wet season is coming. Everyone in Brinegate can feel it — in the way the merchants have begun stacking their stalls with waterproofed covers, in the way the Wall guards stand longer at their posts, scanning the horizon with a particular quality of attention that has nothing to do with smugglers or curfew-breakers. The sea is eight days east and its rhythms have always been felt here, in the hinge between the Middle Tier and the Outer, where the smell of salt is a permanent undercurrent in the wood smoke and tallow.

The Last Sound Inn has been full since yesterday. Travelers who didn't want to be caught in the outer reaches when the season turned have been arriving in small, tense clusters all day. By evening the common room is warm with bodies and dense with the particular conversation of people who are deciding, quietly, whether to be afraid.

---

Four of them share a table near the hearth — strangers by most measures, though something in the way they occupy their space suggests that the evening has already begun to make them something else.

Mara Embervale sits with the particular stillness of someone accustomed to being looked at and having decided not to care. Her horns catch the firelight. She has ordered wine and not yet touched it. There is a quality of listening about her, oriented toward a question the room hasn't asked yet.

Across the table, Wynn is the oldest person in the room by an order of magnitude that he does not advertise. He carries his centuries the way old ships carry barnacles — not as burden, but as evidence of having stayed in the water long enough to accumulate a life. He has a small notebook open before him and is making the kind of notes that are not about anything in particular, and are therefore about everything.

Nyssara's back is to the wall, as it always is. She has the careful neutrality of someone who has trained herself out of every reflex that might reveal she was born knowing which wine glass is for which course. She watches the door. She watches the room. She watches Mara's untouched wine.

Querk has ordered soup and is studying it with the focused attention they give to most things — not rudely, but thoroughly, as if the soup might contain information. They have already asked the innkeeper three questions about local plant life that the innkeeper was not equipped to answer.

---

The door opens at the second hour of evening. The chill that enters is not remarkable — it's that kind of night. But the woman who follows it is.

She is perhaps fifty, perhaps much older; her face has the quality of being well-used rather than simply aged. She moves through the room with a steadiness that parts the crowd without demanding it — not the swagger of someone who expects deference, but the ease of someone who has simply been in more rooms than this one. There is a sealed package under her arm, wrapped in waxed cloth and tied with three separate knots.

She approaches their table. She doesn't sit.

*I need something carried*, she says, *to a graveyard east of the outer gate. Tonight, if possible. I can pay.*

Her name, she tells them when Nyssara asks, is Kisara Solis.

She doesn't explain the package. She doesn't explain why she can't carry it herself. She watches their faces the way someone watches a lock they're deciding whether to trust, and when they agree she gives them the package and a hand-drawn map and is gone before the warmth she brought with her has quite faded from the air.

Three tables away, behind a curtain of ordinary conversation, another set of ears has been paying very close attention.

---

The route to the graveyard runs along the Wall.

This is not, technically, permitted. The Wall is not a ruin or a relic — it is infrastructure, it is military, it is the physical reason the interior tiers exist at all. The guards stationed along its length are not ceremonial. The path they must take runs inside the outer gate and follows the Wall's eastern face for nearly a mile before cutting into the lower districts where the graves are kept.

But the map shows no other way that would not take three hours longer, and the wet season doesn't wait.

They go at dusk, when the light is ambiguous and the shadows of the Wall's towers fall long across the road. The stone here is very old — older than Brinegate, older than the present empire. There are marks in it that none of them can quite interpret. Querk touches the Wall's face as they pass and says nothing, but their expression suggests that the Wall has something to say in return.

The package is under Mara's arm. She hasn't asked what's in it. She has the paladin's particular combination of curiosity and discipline that keeps a question present without making it urgent.

---

The watchtower is squat and practical, three guards at its base, their lanterns cutting small circles in the deepening dark. The road narrows here. There is no way past that isn't noticed.

Wynn produces a small, round stone from his pocket and asks Querk, very quietly, whether they have any particular aptitude for appearing to be a delivery of official correspondence.

What follows is not elegant. It is, however, effective.

The illusion Wynn weaves is of a courier's seal — projected across the package with the light-bending magic that he has had centuries to make look effortless. The distraction comes from Nyssara, who has disappeared into the shadow of the Wall with the completeness of someone who was never there, and who reappears moments later as a creak of wood and a falling lantern forty feet to the north.

The squirrels are Querk's contribution. No one had anticipated the squirrels. The guards, to their credit, respond with professional alarm to the three suddenly-animated rodents who have decided that this particular moment is an excellent time to investigate the guards' pack lunches. Mara walks through the checkpoint with the calm of someone who has absolute faith that the universe will, in this specific instance, cooperate.

She is correct.

---

The vines find them a quarter-mile past the tower.

They are moving through a section of the Wall where something has gone wrong at a cellular level — the stone is cracked and green-stained, and from those cracks something is pushing. Long, dark runners of vegetation have forced themselves into the mortar and are pulling at it with a persistence that reads, even from twenty feet away, as fury.

It is the Leviathan-bled soil, Querk says quietly. The plants that grow in it grow wrong — too fast, too hungry, shaped by a trace of something that was never meant to become earth at all.

The vines are not interested in the party. They are interested in the Wall.

Wynn begins to play before anyone has decided this is the right approach. The sound he makes is not a song exactly — it is something older than notation, a thing passed from teacher to student by ear across a span of time long enough to have lost its name. The vines slow. They do not stop. But they slow.

Then Mara's foot finds the root that Querk hadn't seen, and the plants that have been slowing down find something new to close around.

The tangle is fast and absolute. For a moment — three seconds, perhaps five — no amount of Wynn's music seems to reach the thing that has her. Her hands get free. The horn catches. She has the presence of mind not to scream.

Querk and Nyssara and Wynn move as one. The vines release her when the music finds the right note — a long, descending phrase that seems to travel down through the roots themselves. She is breathing when they reach her. She is bleeding. She does not complain.

They walk faster after that.

---

The graveyard is small and old, contained behind an iron fence that has grown into the trees on either side. It is the kind of place that exists in most outer-tier cities — kept for the adventurers who come to the Wall's edge and don't make it home, tended by someone whose name no one quite remembers now.

A figure is waiting among the headstones.

The shape of her is right — the height, the steadiness, the way she stands near a particular grave with both hands clasped before her. The package that Mara is carrying is correct. The meeting is correct.

But something is not.

Nyssara feels it first, as she usually does: a wrongness in the shadows behind the figure, a quality in the light at her edges that has nothing to do with the moon. She says nothing yet. She watches.

Wynn feels it second — not as a visual disturbance but as a kind of narrative incorrectness, the feeling a very old story-keeper gets when a sentence is constructed by someone who knows the words but not the rhythm.

Mara is the one who says, quietly: *That's not her.*

---

The figure doesn't break immediately. There is a moment of professional appraisal — the wizard who has been maintaining the illusion has been doing this long enough to know that the jig is up, and to consider whether there is anything to be gained from continuing. There is not.

The illusion falls. Underneath it: a person in travel-worn clothes with the look of someone who has been awake for thirty hours and has stopped enjoying their choices. He holds his hands out. *I'm not interested in a fight*, he says. *The package was the job. Someone hired us.*

Then the gate opens behind them, and the real Kisara Solis walks in.

She is not in a hurry. She takes in the scene — the rival wizard, the four strangers she hired, the headstones — with the expression of someone watching a play they have seen before and are curious to see how this cast handles it.

*They didn't open it*, Nyssara says. Not a question.

*No*, Kisara agrees. *They didn't.*

She looks at each of them in turn — really looks, with the kind of attention that counts things. *I owe you an explanation*, she says. *Actually — I owe you a confession.*

The graveyard is quiet around them. The vines to the north have gone still. Somewhere behind them, the guards at the watchtower are presumably still dealing with the aftermath of the squirrels.

*I'm tired*, Kisara says. *I've been tired for a long time. I wanted to see if you were worth trusting before I told you why.*

---

The rest of the rival party arrives before she is finished speaking.

There are four of them. They have the look of people who planned to outnumber a situation, and who are currently recalculating, having found that the situation contains more variables than anticipated. Their leader — a woman with close-cropped hair and a scar along her jaw — takes in the scene in half a second and makes a decision.

*Give us the package*, she says. *And there's no trouble.*

The trouble, as it turns out, is unavoidable.

What follows in the graveyard is quick and ugly and does not go well for the rivals. This is not because the party is larger or better-equipped — they are neither — but because they have spent the last three hours becoming, without choosing to, a unit. The way that Mara moves toward danger creates space. The way Nyssara uses that space has a quality of inevitability. Wynn does not fight, exactly, but what he does with sound and story and the long training of a very old person keeps the variables manageable. Querk, when the moment requires it, is not kind.

Two of the rivals do not get up. The other two are breathing and persuadable by the time Nyssara makes it clear — with quiet precision — exactly what the alternative looks like.

*Go*, Mara tells them.

They go.

---

Kisara opens the package herself.

Inside: a mushroom. It is wrong in the way the Wall's vines were wrong — too deliberate, too aware. It grows in a glass vial packed with cotton, and it pulses with something that is not quite bioluminescence. Mycelial, she says. Capable of allowing communion with the recently departed — or, with sufficient time and care, something closer to revival. Something that remembers being the person it was. Something that still loves, in the way a translation still means.

*My partner*, she says. *He died here. Three years ago, on this side of the Wall.* She does not look at the nearest grave, but they all know which one she means.

*What this would bring back*, she continues, *would not be him. It would be something that remembered being him. I've known that since I found it. I've been carrying it anyway.*

She holds the vial and looks at it for a long time. The night has gotten very quiet.

Wynn speaks first, because he has been alive long enough to know which silences should be broken and which held, and this one has been held long enough. What he says is not a comfort, exactly — it is an accounting. Something about the nature of loss: how the dead become two things at once, the person they were and the idea of the person, and how the idea persists regardless of mushrooms or prayers or the particular cruelty of the wet season approaching again.

*You've been coming to the Wall every day*, Mara says. It isn't a question.

Kisara nods.

*I want to stop*, she says. *I want to be done with it. I want to build something that faces forward.* She looks at all four of them with the clear, exhausted eyes of someone who has finally said the true thing. *I wanted to know if you were people I could build it with.*

She sets the vial down on the grave. The mushroom pulses once. Then the light fades, and does not return.

They walk back through the Wall's shadow toward Brinegate, where, somewhere in the city's unfinished districts, a guild hall has not yet been named — and is waiting to be.
