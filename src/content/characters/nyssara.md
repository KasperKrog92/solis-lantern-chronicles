Nyssara came to Brinegate the way a lot of people come to Brinegate — through the gate, with a bag that contained everything she owned and a name she was hoping no one had yet heard. The name, in her case, is a problem: her family is well-known in the lower reaches of inner-tier society, and their reputation follows their bloodline with the tenacity of a good debt collector.

She has spent three years building something of her own in the city. It is not criminal, exactly — she draws a careful distinction between operating in the grey margins of the law and operating against it — but it is extensive. She knows people. She knows which people know people. She knows which gates open at what hours and which guards prefer to be paid in information rather than coin. This knowledge is its own kind of wealth, and she is methodical about accumulating it.

What she is less methodical about: why she stayed in Brinegate when she could have gone further. Why she agreed to Kisara's errand before the money was fully discussed. Why she noticed, that night in the graveyard, that Mara didn't ask for help when the vines had her — and why that particular observation settled somewhere in her memory with unusual weight.

She suspects the guild will complicate her careful arrangements. She has decided this is probably acceptable.
