Wynn does not discuss exactly how old he is, and the few people who have pressed him on the subject have found the conversation redirected with a fluency that suggests long practice. What is apparent is that he has been in Brinegate for several decades, that he arrived from somewhere far enough east that the details have grown abstract, and that in that time he has accumulated an informal archive of stories — written, spoken, remembered — that takes up most of two rooms in the apartment above the instrument shop where he works most mornings.

He plays several instruments well and one instrument extraordinarily. The extraordinary instrument does not have a conventional name. He built it himself, over a period of years, and the sound it makes in the right hands (his hands) has been described variously as: a human voice translated into weather, a conversation between two rivers, the sound a story makes when it is finally told to the right person. The vines responded to it. Most things do, eventually.

Wynn joined the guild because Kisara asked, and because he has reached the age where the difference between watching history and participating in it seems less and less significant. He keeps his notebook. He writes things down. He believes, more than he would say aloud, that what happens next in Brinegate is worth recording carefully.
